# Playbook: Marketing Psychology — The Top 1% Mental Models

## Why This Playbook Exists
The top 1% of marketers don't just know tactics — they understand the psychology behind why people buy. This playbook encodes the 50+ most powerful psychological principles used in marketing, organized by where they apply in the customer journey. A solo founder armed with these mental models can outperform teams 10x their size.

---

## Part 1: Attention & Awareness Psychology

### 1.1 Pattern Interrupt
**What:** The brain filters 99% of stimuli. To be noticed, you must break the expected pattern.
**Application:** Open with something unexpected — a contrarian claim, surprising stat, or visual disruption.
**Example:** "Stop creating content calendars" (when everyone says to create one).

### 1.2 The Cocktail Party Effect
**What:** People unconsciously tune into information relevant to them, even in noisy environments.
**Application:** Use the reader's identity language in headlines: their job title, industry, pain point, or aspiration.
**Example:** "Solo founders: here's what $500K/year marketers know that you don't."

### 1.3 Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
**What:** Items that stand out from their surroundings are more easily remembered.
**Application:** Make ONE thing visually or conceptually different. Highlight your key message. Use contrast in design.
**Example:** In a world of blue SaaS logos, choose orange. In a world of jargon, use plain language.

### 1.4 Zeigarnik Effect
**What:** People remember uncompleted tasks better than completed ones. The brain craves closure.
**Application:** Open loops in content — start a story but delay the resolution. Use "wait for it..." or "the last one surprised me."
**Example:** "I'll share the #1 tactic that generated $200K at the end, but first..."

### 1.5 Curiosity Gap
**What:** When there's a gap between what we know and what we want to know, we're compelled to close it.
**Application:** Headlines that promise information but require a click to complete the thought.
**Example:** "The pricing mistake that cost us $47K (and how we fixed it in 24 hours)."

## Part 2: Trust & Credibility Psychology

### 2.1 Social Proof (Cialdini)
**What:** People follow the actions of others, especially when uncertain.
**Application:** Show numbers (users, customers, reviews), testimonials, logos, case studies, "most popular" badges.
**Hierarchy of power:**
1. Named person with photo, title, and company (strongest)
2. Aggregated numbers ("10,000+ founders")
3. Brand logos
4. Star ratings
5. Anonymous testimonials (weakest — avoid if possible)

### 2.2 Authority Principle (Cialdini)
**What:** People defer to experts and those with perceived authority.
**Application:** Display credentials, media mentions ("as seen in"), expert endorsements, author bios, certifications.
**Example:** "Built on frameworks from 15 marketing masters including Schwartz, Hormozi, and Dunford."

### 2.3 The Halo Effect
**What:** A positive impression in one area creates a positive impression in others.
**Application:** If your design looks professional, people assume your product is professional. If your writing is sharp, they assume your thinking is sharp.
**Implication:** Invest in design quality — it signals product quality.

### 2.4 Mere Exposure Effect
**What:** People develop a preference for things simply because they're familiar with them.
**Application:** Consistent presence across channels. Show up regularly. Retarget. Email consistently. The more they see you, the more they trust you.
**Rule:** 7-13 touchpoints before a purchase decision is typical.

### 2.5 The Ben Franklin Effect
**What:** People who do you a favor are MORE likely to do another favor (and buy) than those you've done a favor for.
**Application:** Ask for small commitments first: "What do you think of this?" → "Would you share this?" → "Want to try it?"

### 2.6 Trust Transfer
**What:** Trust from a known entity transfers to an unknown entity through association.
**Application:** Partner with trusted brands, get endorsements from respected people, get featured in trusted media, display "as seen in" logos.

## Part 3: Desire & Motivation Psychology

### 3.1 Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)
**What:** The pain of losing is 2x more powerful than the pleasure of gaining.
**Application:** Frame your message in terms of what they'll LOSE by not acting, not just what they'll gain.
**Example:** Instead of "Save 10 hours/week" → "You're losing 520 hours per year to manual marketing."

### 3.2 The Endowment Effect
**What:** People value things more once they feel ownership over them.
**Application:** Free trials, interactive demos, personalization. Once they set up their account, they don't want to lose their data/progress.
**Example:** "Your personalized marketing plan is ready" (they now "own" it).

### 3.3 IKEA Effect
**What:** People value things more when they've invested effort in creating them.
**Application:** Let users customize, configure, or build something during onboarding. The more they invest, the more they value it.
**Example:** "Build your marketing profile" → now they're invested.

### 3.4 Scarcity Principle (Cialdini)
**What:** Things perceived as scarce are valued more highly.
**Application:** Limited-time offers, limited seats, limited beta access, "only X spots left."
**Warning:** Only use genuine scarcity. Fake countdown timers destroy trust permanently.

### 3.5 Anchoring Effect
**What:** The first piece of information sets the reference point for all subsequent judgment.
**Application:** Show the high-value price first, then reveal your price. Show "enterprise teams pay $2,000/month for this. You get it for $59."
**Example:** Pricing pages that show the most expensive tier first.

### 3.6 The Contrast Principle
**What:** Things appear different when presented next to something else.
**Application:** Show the "before" (pain, manual work, high cost) right before the "after" (your solution). The contrast amplifies the value.
**Example:** Side-by-side comparison: "Hiring a marketing team: $120K/year. Marketing36z OS: $59/month."

## Part 4: Decision & Conversion Psychology

### 4.1 The Paradox of Choice (Barry Schwartz)
**What:** Too many options cause decision paralysis and buyer's remorse.
**Application:** Limit choices. 3 pricing tiers, not 5. One primary CTA, not three. One recommended option, clearly highlighted.
**Rule:** If you offer more than 3 options, reduce or use progressive disclosure.

### 4.2 Default Effect
**What:** People tend to go with the pre-selected or recommended option.
**Application:** Pre-select the pricing tier you want most customers to choose. Default trial to annual billing. Pre-check the "recommended" features.

### 4.3 The Decoy Effect
**What:** Adding a clearly inferior option makes a target option look more attractive.
**Application:** Three pricing tiers where the middle tier is clearly the best value because the lowest tier is too limited and the highest is too expensive.

### 4.4 Commitment & Consistency (Cialdini)
**What:** Once people commit to something (even small), they're more likely to continue in that direction.
**Application:** Get micro-commitments: newsletter signup → free tool → webinar → trial → paid. Each step makes the next more likely.
**The foot-in-the-door technique:** Ask for something small first, then gradually increase.

### 4.5 Reciprocity Principle (Cialdini)
**What:** When someone gives us something valuable, we feel obligated to reciprocate.
**Application:** Give genuine value first — free tools, free templates, free advice, free trials. The more value you give, the more obligation they feel.
**Rule:** Give before you ask. Always.

### 4.6 Zero-Risk Bias
**What:** People strongly prefer options that eliminate risk entirely over those that merely reduce it.
**Application:** "Money-back guarantee" is good. "Full refund + keep the product" is better. "We'll pay you $100 if it doesn't work" is best.

### 4.7 Status Quo Bias
**What:** People prefer the current state of affairs. Change requires effort.
**Application:** Make switching as easy as possible. Offer migration assistance. Show the cost of inaction (what they lose by staying with the status quo).

## Part 5: Retention & Loyalty Psychology

### 5.1 The Peak-End Rule
**What:** People judge experiences based on the peak (most intense) moment and the end.
**Application:** Create a "wow moment" during onboarding (the peak). End every interaction on a positive note. The experience between peaks matters less than you think.

### 5.2 Variable Reward Schedule (Skinner)
**What:** Unpredictable rewards are more engaging than predictable ones.
**Application:** Surprise customers with unexpected value: bonus features, personal thank-you notes, early access to new features, exclusive content.

### 5.3 The Sunk Cost Effect
**What:** People continue investing in something because of previously invested resources.
**Application:** Help users build data, workflows, and habits within your product. The more they invest, the harder it is to leave.
**Ethical note:** Build genuine value, not artificial lock-in.

### 5.4 Identity-Based Loyalty
**What:** When a product becomes part of someone's identity, they become evangelists.
**Application:** Help customers see themselves as the kind of person who uses your product. "You're not just using a tool — you're a marketing-first founder."

## Part 6: Pricing Psychology

### 6.1 Charm Pricing
**What:** Prices ending in 9 or 7 are perceived as significantly lower.
**Application:** $49 feels much less than $50. $97 feels less than $100. Use for consumer/SMB pricing.
**Exception:** Luxury/premium brands use round numbers ($100, $500) to signal quality.

### 6.2 Price-Quality Heuristic
**What:** People assume higher price = higher quality.
**Application:** Don't race to the bottom on price. If you're the cheapest, people wonder what's wrong. Price to signal value.

### 6.3 The Weber-Fechner Law
**What:** Perception of change is proportional to the starting point.
**Application:** A $10 increase on a $100 product feels significant. The same $10 increase on a $1,000 product feels trivial. Use for upsell framing.

### 6.4 Payment Decoupling
**What:** Separating payment from consumption reduces the "pain of paying."
**Application:** Annual billing (pay once, use all year), subscription models, "set it and forget it" framing.

## Part 7: Persuasion Frameworks (Combining Multiple Principles)

### The CLASP Framework (for any marketing message)
| Letter | Principle | Application |
|:------:|-----------|------------|
| **C** | Credibility | Establish authority and trust first |
| **L** | Logic | Present rational evidence and data |
| **A** | Appeal | Connect emotionally to desires/fears |
| **S** | Scarcity | Create genuine urgency to act |
| **P** | Proof | Show social validation from peers |

### The "Narrative Transportation" Effect
When people are absorbed in a story, they become less critical and more persuadable. Stories reduce counter-arguing.

**Application to marketing:**
1. Start with a character the reader identifies with
2. Introduce a problem they recognize
3. Build tension (what happens if unsolved)
4. Introduce the epiphany/solution
5. Show the transformation
6. Bridge to the reader's situation

This is why case studies and founder stories outperform feature lists every time.
