## Part 1: The Three Pricing Axes

*Source: MarketingSkills `pricing-strategy` skill*

Every pricing decision sits at the intersection of three axes:

| Axis | Question | Options |
|------|----------|---------|
| **1. Packaging** | How do you bundle value? | Good-Better-Best tiers, à la carte, platform + add-ons |
| **2. Pricing Metric** | What do you charge per? | Per user/seat, per usage, per feature, per contact, per transaction, flat fee |
| **3. Price Point** | How much? | Van Westendorp research, value-based calculation, competitive anchoring |

**Value metric test:** "As the customer uses more, do they get more value?" If yes → that's your value metric.

| Metric Type | Best For | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| Per seat | Collaboration tools, team products | "$X/user/month" |
| Per usage | Volume-driven products | "$X per 1,000 API calls" |
| Per feature | Products with distinct capability tiers | "Analytics available on Pro+" |
| Flat fee | Simple products, early-stage | "$49/month, everything included" |

**Price increase strategies:**
1. Grandfather existing customers at their current rate
2. Delayed increase with 30-60 day notice
3. Tied to new value added ("We're raising prices because we added X, Y, Z")
4. Plan restructure (reorganize tiers rather than raw increase)

## Part 1b: The Pricing Foundation

### The Core Pricing Principle

> **Price is what you pay. Value is what you get.** — Warren Buffett

Most founders underprice because they anchor to cost, not value. The Marketing36z approach: price based on the value of the OUTCOME you deliver, not the cost of the INPUTS you provide.

### The 3 Pricing Strategies

| Strategy | When to Use | Risk | Example |
|----------|------------|------|---------|
| **Cost-Plus** | Commodities, physical products | Low margin, race to bottom | Materials ($10) + markup (50%) = $15 |
| **Competitor-Based** | Established markets, me-too products | No differentiation | "Others charge $99, we charge $79" |
| **Value-Based** | Differentiated products, clear outcomes | Requires proof of value | "We save you $50K/year, investment is $5K" |

**Rule:** Always aim for value-based pricing. Cost-plus and competitor-based are fallback positions, not strategies.

## Part 2: The Value-Based Pricing Process

### Step 1: Quantify the Value You Create

Calculate the economic value of your product's outcome:

| Value Dimension | Question | Calculation |
|----------------|----------|-------------|
| **Revenue increase** | How much additional revenue does your product generate? | $ per month/year |
| **Cost reduction** | What costs does it eliminate? | $ saved per month/year |
| **Time savings** | How many hours does it save? | Hours × hourly rate |
| **Risk reduction** | What costly mistakes does it prevent? | Expected value of prevented losses |
| **Opportunity cost** | What becomes possible that wasn't before? | Value of new capability |

**Total quantifiable value:** $___/year

**Pricing rule of thumb:** Charge 10-20% of the value you create. If you save them $50K/year, a $5K-10K price is a no-brainer.

### Step 2: The Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Analysis

Ask your ICP these 4 questions:

1. "At what price would this be so cheap you'd question the quality?" → **Too Cheap**
2. "At what price would this be a great deal — a bargain?" → **Cheap/Good Value**
3. "At what price would this be getting expensive but you'd still consider it?" → **Expensive but Acceptable**
4. "At what price would this be too expensive to even consider?" → **Too Expensive**

| Question | Average Response |
|----------|:---------------:|
| Too Cheap | $ |
| Good Value | $ |
| Expensive but Acceptable | $ |
| Too Expensive | $ |

**Optimal price range:** Between "Good Value" and "Expensive but Acceptable"

### Step 3: Apply the Hormozi Value Equation

Your price must be justified by the Value Equation score:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
```

| Factor | Score (1-10) | How to Improve |
|--------|:-----------:|---------------|
| **Dream Outcome** | | Make the promised result bigger and more specific |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | | Add proof, guarantees, testimonials |
| **Time Delay** | | Speed up time to first result |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** | | Remove steps, do more for them |

**If Value Equation score is <5.0:** Fix the offer before raising the price.
**If score is >7.0:** You can likely charge more than you think.

## Part 3: Pricing Architecture

### Tiered Pricing Design

Most products benefit from 3 tiers. Each tier serves a different buyer:

| Tier | Purpose | Buyer Type | Pricing Anchor |
|------|---------|-----------|:-------------:|
| **Starter** | Entry point, proves value | Budget-conscious, testing | 1x |
| **Growth** (Most Popular) | Full solution, best value | Core ICP, serious buyer | 2-3x Starter |
| **Premium** | Maximum outcome, white glove | High-value, wants everything | 3-5x Starter |

### The Decoy Effect

The middle tier should look like the obvious best value:

```
Starter: $29/mo        Growth: $59/mo ← BEST VALUE       Premium: $149/mo
- Feature A             - Everything in Starter             - Everything in Growth
- Feature B             - Feature C                         - Feature F
- 1 user                - Feature D                         - Feature G
                        - Feature E                         - Dedicated support
                        - 3 users                           - Unlimited users
```

**Design principle:** The gap between Starter and Growth should feel small relative to the value added. The gap between Growth and Premium should feel large.

### Feature Gating vs. Usage Gating

| Gating Method | Best For | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **Feature-based** | Products with clearly differentiated capabilities | Basic: Reports. Pro: Reports + API + Integrations |
| **Usage-based** | Products where value scales with volume | Free: 100 contacts. Pro: 10K contacts. Enterprise: Unlimited |
| **Outcome-based** | Services and consulting | Starter: Template. Pro: Template + review. Premium: Done-for-you |
| **Support-based** | Products where guidance matters | Self-serve vs. onboarding call vs. dedicated CSM |

## Part 4: Pricing Psychology

### The 9 Pricing Principles

1. **Anchor high, then show the deal**
   - Show the full value first ("Worth $2,400"), then the price ("Yours for $497")

2. **Price in the currency of their budget**
   - B2B: Annual cost. B2C: Daily cost. ("Less than your daily coffee — $1.63/day")

3. **Remove the dollar sign on menus/pricing pages**
   - Studies show prices without $ symbols feel less painful

4. **Use charm pricing for consumer ($29 vs. $30)**
   - Exception: premium/luxury products round UP ($300, not $299)

5. **Annual vs. monthly framing**
   - Show monthly price, bill annually: "$49/mo (billed annually at $468 — save $120)"

6. **The "compared to what?" frame**
   - Compare to: a hire ($5K/mo), an agency ($3K/mo), the cost of the problem ($X/mo lost)

7. **Price certainty reduces anxiety**
   - Fixed price > hourly. Package > scope-based. Flat rate > variable.

8. **The takeaway close**
   - "This isn't for everyone. If you're not ready to [commitment], this isn't the right fit."

9. **Justify the premium, don't apologize for it**
   - "We're more expensive because [specific reason]. Here's why that matters to your results."

## Part 5: Guarantee & Risk Reversal Strategy

### The Guarantee Menu (Jay Abraham)

| Guarantee Type | Risk Level (for you) | Conversion Impact | When to Use |
|---------------|:-------------------:|:-----------------:|-------------|
| **Satisfaction guarantee** | Low | Medium | Default for most products |
| **Money-back guarantee** | Medium | High | When you're confident in delivery |
| **Results guarantee** | High | Very High | When you can measure outcomes |
| **Better-than-money-back** | Very High | Highest | When you want to dominate (refund + keep bonuses) |
| **Performance guarantee** | Variable | High | B2B services with measurable KPIs |
| **Conditional guarantee** | Low-Medium | Medium-High | "If you do X, Y, Z and don't see results..." |

### Guarantee Design Template

```
If you [specific action/commitment] and don't see [specific measurable result]
within [specific timeframe], we will [specific remedy].

No questions asked / After reviewing your implementation / With proof of completion.
```

### When NOT to Guarantee
- When you can't control the outcome (customer effort matters)
- When abuse potential is high (digital products with instant access)
- When the price point doesn't justify it (<$20 purchases rarely need guarantees)

## Part 6: Pricing Experiments

### The Testing Protocol

| Experiment | How to Run | Sample Size | Decision Criteria |
|-----------|-----------|:-----------:|------------------|
| **A/B price test** | Show different prices to different cohorts | 100+ per variant | Revenue per visitor (not just conversion rate) |
| **Price page layout** | Test tier presentation, anchoring, feature display | 200+ per variant | Conversion rate + average deal size |
| **Discount vs. bonus** | 20% off vs. added bonus of equal value | 100+ per variant | Conversion rate + perceived value |
| **Annual vs. monthly** | Default to annual vs. default to monthly | 200+ per variant | LTV and churn rate |
| **Guarantee test** | With guarantee vs. without | 100+ per variant | Net revenue after refunds |

### The Price Increase Playbook

When it's time to raise prices:

1. **Grandfather existing customers** (at their current rate, or with a smaller increase)
2. **Add value before raising price** (new feature, better support, added resource)
3. **Announce in advance** (30-60 days notice)
4. **Frame as investment in improvement** ("We're investing in [X] to deliver better results")
5. **Offer a lock-in option** ("Upgrade to annual now to lock in the current rate")
