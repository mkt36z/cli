# Playbook: Positioning Masterclass

## Sources
- April Dunford — *Obviously Awesome*

## Why This Exists
Positioning is the single most important strategic decision in marketing. It determines everything downstream — messaging, pricing, channels, competitive framing, and customer perception. Get positioning wrong and no amount of great copy will save you. Get it right and mediocre copy can still work.

## The April Dunford Positioning Process (10-Step Deep Dive)

### Step 1: Understand What You're Really Selling
Most founders describe what their product IS. Positioning starts with understanding what it DOES for the customer.

**Exercise: The "So What?" Ladder**
```
We have [feature] → So what?
That means [benefit] → So what?
Which lets you [outcome] → So what?
So your life is [transformation] ← THIS is what you're selling
```

Example:
- "We have AI-powered analytics" → So what?
- "That means you see trends before competitors" → So what?
- "Which lets you make better bets on product features" → So what?
- "So you stop wasting months building things nobody wants" ← Sell THIS

### Step 2: List Your Competitive Alternatives (Not Just Competitors)
Competitive alternatives are NOT just direct competitors. They include:
- **Direct competitors** (similar products in same category)
- **Indirect competitors** (different products solving same problem)
- **DIY solutions** (spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring someone)
- **Do nothing** (live with the problem — this is your biggest competitor)

**Critical insight:** If your customers would "do nothing" as their alternative, you have a positioning problem — they don't believe the problem is worth solving. You must first position the PROBLEM as urgent before positioning your SOLUTION.

### Step 3: Identify Your Unique Attributes
What do you have that your competitive alternatives DON'T? Be brutally honest.

**Categories of unique attributes:**
- Features or capabilities competitors lack
- Technical architecture advantages (speed, reliability, scalability)
- Business model advantages (pricing, packaging, delivery)
- Team advantages (expertise, experience, access)
- Data advantages (proprietary data, network effects)
- Ecosystem advantages (integrations, partnerships)
- Speed advantages (time to value, implementation speed)

**Rule:** If a competitor also has it, it's not unique. If every product in the category has it, it's table stakes. Only list what's genuinely differentiated.

### Step 4: Map Attributes to Value
For each unique attribute, define:

| Unique Attribute | Value It Delivers | Who Cares Most |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| [Feature X] | [Specific outcome] | [Customer type] |

**The value must be a CUSTOMER outcome, not a product capability.**
- BAD: "AI-powered" (that's a feature)
- GOOD: "Finds profitable keywords in 10 seconds instead of 4 hours" (that's value)

### Step 5: Identify Your Best-Fit Customer
Not everyone in your market cares about your unique value equally. Find the customers who care the MOST.

**Best-fit customer characteristics:**
- The problem you solve is their #1 or #2 priority (not #10)
- They have budget and authority to buy
- They get the most value from your unique attributes
- They're reachable through channels you can afford
- They can become advocates (willing to refer, review, case study)

**Warning signs of BAD-fit customers:**
- They need heavy customization to find value
- They churn within 90 days
- They generate the most support tickets
- They negotiate hardest on price
- They never become advocates

### Step 6: Choose Your Market Category
The market category is the mental box customers put you in. It determines:
- What they compare you to
- What they expect you to do
- What they're willing to pay
- How they find you

**Three positioning strategies:**

| Strategy | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Head-to-head** | You can win on the existing criteria | "CRM for sales teams — but 10x faster" |
| **Subcategory** | The market is big but you serve a niche better | "CRM for solo consultants" |
| **New category** | You're genuinely doing something different | "Revenue intelligence platform" (not CRM) |

### Step 7: Layer on Relevant Trends (Optional)
If a genuine market trend makes your positioning more compelling, reference it.

**Rules for trends:**
- The trend must be REAL and understood by your audience
- It must make your positioning more believable, not more confusing
- Never build positioning ON a trend (trends fade) — use trends to reinforce positioning

Examples:
- "Built for the AI-first marketing team" (if AI is reshaping your customers' workflow)
- "The PLG platform for the async-work era" (if remote work is a genuine driver)

### Step 8: Capture Your Positioning Statement

**Template:**
```
For [best-fit customer]
who [need/job to be done],
[Product Name] is a [market category]
that [key value proposition].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
we [primary differentiator].
```

**Rules:**
- One sentence for each line
- Must be TRUE (no aspirational claims)
- Must be SPECIFIC (no "best" or "leading")
- A new employee should be able to recite it after reading once

### Step 9: Test Your Positioning

**The Positioning Stress Test:**
1. Show it to 5 ideal customers. Can they immediately understand what you do and why it matters?
2. Show it to 5 employees. Would they describe the product the same way?
3. Show it to 5 prospects who chose a competitor. Does this change their perception?
4. Show it to someone who knows nothing about your market. Can they explain it back to you?

**If it fails any test:** Iterate. Positioning is not set-and-forget — it evolves as you learn.

### Step 10: Align Everything to Positioning

Once positioning is locked, EVERYTHING must align:

| Element | Must Reflect Positioning |
|---------|------------------------|
| Website messaging | Headlines, subheads, value props |
| Sales conversations | Discovery questions, pitch, objection handling |
| Content marketing | Topics, angles, expertise areas |
| Product decisions | Feature priorities, roadmap focus |
| Pricing | Must match perceived market category |
| Hiring | Team skills must support differentiation |
| Partnerships | Partners must reinforce market category |

## Ries & Trout: The Laws of Positioning

### Law of Leadership
It's better to be first in a category than to be better in an existing category. If you can't be first, create a new category where you ARE first.

### Law of the Mind
It's better to be first in the mind than first in the market. Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products.

### Law of Focus
Own a word in the prospect's mind. Volvo = safety. FedEx = overnight. Your brand must = ONE concept.

**Exercise:** Complete this sentence: "[Your brand] = ___________"
If you can't fill it in with ONE word or concept, your positioning isn't focused enough.

### Law of the Opposite
If you're #2, don't try to be a better version of #1. Be the OPPOSITE. If they're big and corporate, be small and agile. If they're feature-rich, be simple. If they're expensive, be accessible.

### Law of Division
Over time, every category divides into subcategories. Position yourself to own a subcategory before it fully emerges.
