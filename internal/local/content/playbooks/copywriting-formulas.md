## Part 1: Headline Formulas

### The 25 Proven Headline Frameworks

Every headline must accomplish ONE of four goals: make a promise, arouse curiosity, state a benefit, or acknowledge a pain.

#### Promise Headlines
```
1. "How to [Get Desired Outcome] Without [Biggest Objection]"
   → "How to Double Your Email List Without Spending a Dollar on Ads"

2. "The [Number]-Step System for [Getting Desired Outcome] in [Timeframe]"
   → "The 5-Step System for Closing $10K Deals in 14 Days"

3. "[Number] Ways to [Desired Outcome] [Faster/Better/Easier]"
   → "7 Ways to Write Landing Pages That Convert 3x Better"

4. "Get [Specific Result] — Guaranteed, or [Strong Risk Reversal]"
   → "Get 1,000 New Email Subscribers in 30 Days — Guaranteed, or Your Money Back"

5. "The Lazy [Person's] Way to [Desired Outcome]"
   → "The Lazy Marketer's Way to Create 30 Days of Content in One Afternoon"
```

#### Curiosity Headlines
```
6. "What [Unexpected Group] Can Teach You About [Relevant Topic]"
   → "What Stand-Up Comedians Can Teach You About Writing Hooks"

7. "The [Adjective] Truth About [Common Belief]"
   → "The Uncomfortable Truth About Content Marketing ROI"

8. "[Number] [Things] You Didn't Know About [Topic] (Number [X] Will [Emotion])"
   → "5 Things You Didn't Know About LinkedIn's Algorithm (Number 3 Will Change How You Post)"

9. "Why [Counterintuitive Statement]"
   → "Why Your Best Customers Are the Ones Who Almost Didn't Buy"

10. "The [Industry/Topic] Secret That [Impressive Group] Don't Want You to Know"
    → "The Pricing Secret That $100M Companies Don't Want You to Know"
```

#### Benefit Headlines
```
11. "[Do Something] Like [Aspirational Identity]"
    → "Write Headlines Like a $50K/Month Copywriter"

12. "Finally — [Solution to Chronic Problem]"
    → "Finally — A CRM That Solo Founders Can Actually Set Up in an Afternoon"

13. "Announcing: [Specific New Thing That Solves Problem]"
    → "Announcing: The First AI That Writes Landing Pages at a 10%+ Conversion Rate"

14. "[Achieve Result] in [Short Time] — Even If [Common Disadvantage]"
    → "Build Your First Funnel in 2 Hours — Even If You've Never Written a Line of Code"

15. "Stop [Painful Activity]. Start [Desirable Activity]."
    → "Stop Guessing What to Post. Start Creating Content That Converts."
```

#### Pain/Problem Headlines
```
16. "Are You Making These [Number] [Topic] Mistakes?"
    → "Are You Making These 5 Email Marketing Mistakes?"

17. "Warning: [Threat Related to What They Care About]"
    → "Warning: Your Landing Page Is Losing 67% of Visitors Before They Scroll"

18. "If [Relatable Situation], Here's What to Do"
    → "If Your Ads Are Getting Clicks but No Sales, Here's What to Do"

19. "[Painful Thing] Is Costing You [Something They Value]"
    → "Your Slow Follow-Up Is Costing You $4,200 Per Month in Lost Deals"

20. "Why [Thing They're Doing] Isn't Working (And What to Do Instead)"
    → "Why Your Content Strategy Isn't Working (And What to Do Instead)"
```

#### Authority/Proof Headlines
```
21. "How I [Achieved Specific Result] in [Timeframe] (Exact Process)"
    → "How I Grew From 0 to 10,000 Subscribers in 90 Days (Exact Process)"

22. "I Tested [Number] [Things] — Here's What Actually Works"
    → "I Tested 47 LinkedIn Hook Formats — Here's What Actually Gets Engagement"

23. "[Credible Source] Says [Surprising Claim] — Here's What It Means for You"
    → "Harvard Research Says 95% of Decisions Are Emotional — Here's What It Means for Your Copy"

24. "[Known Person/Brand] Used This [Strategy] to [Impressive Result]"
    → "How Stripe Used This Onboarding Tactic to Reach $95B Valuation"

25. "What [Number] [Impressive Achievement] Taught Me About [Topic]"
    → "What Sending 10,000 Cold Emails Taught Me About First Impressions"
```

## Part 2: Body Copy Structures

### Structure 1: AIDA (Classic, adapted)
```
Attention   → Hook (headline + opening line)
Interest    → Build intrigue (story, data, or problem agitation)
Desire      → Create want (benefits, proof, transformation)
Action      → Call to action (specific, urgent, risk-reversed)
```

### Structure 2: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)
```
Problem     → Name the specific problem they're facing
Agitate     → Make the problem feel urgent and painful (consequences of NOT solving)
Solve       → Present your solution as the clear answer
```

**Extended version — PASO (Problem-Agitate-Solve-Outcome):**
Add an Outcome section showing the transformation after the solution.

### Structure 3: Before-After-Bridge (BAB)
```
Before      → Paint their current painful reality (use THEIR words)
After       → Paint the dream state after the problem is solved
Bridge      → Show how your product/service is the bridge between the two
```

### Structure 4: The Star-Chain-Hook (Gary Halbert)
```
Star        → Open with something spectacular (attention-grabbing star of the show)
Chain       → Build a chain of facts, benefits, and proof that creates desire
Hook        → Close with an irresistible hook (offer + urgency + guarantee)
```

### Structure 5: The 4P's (Dan Kennedy)
```
Promise     → Open with the big promise (what they'll get)
Picture     → Paint a vivid picture of their life with the result
Proof       → Stack evidence that the promise is credible
Push        → Push them to take action NOW
```

### Structure 6: The 1-2-3-4 Formula
```
1. Here's what I've got for you
2. Here's what it will do for you
3. Here's who I am (why you should listen)
4. Here's what you need to do next
```

### Structure 7: The Sugarman Slippery Slide
Every element of copy has one purpose: to get you to read the NEXT line.
- Headline's job → get you to read the subheadline
- Subheadline's job → get you to read the first paragraph
- First paragraph's job → get you to read the second paragraph
- Every paragraph → keeps the slide going until the CTA

**Key technique:** Start with an incomplete thought, curiosity loop, or surprising statement that can only be resolved by reading further.

### Structure 8: Epiphany Bridge Story (Brunson)
```
1. The Backstory     → Where were you before the epiphany?
2. The Journey       → What were you struggling with?
3. The Wall          → What moment of frustration or failure hit?
4. The Epiphany      → What sudden realization changed everything?
5. The Plan          → What new approach did you take?
6. The Transformation → What specific results did you get?
7. The Bridge        → How can THEY experience the same transformation?
```

## Part 3: Schwartz's Headline Strategy by Awareness Level

### Level 1: Unaware
The prospect doesn't even know they have a problem.

**Headline strategy:** Lead with identification or story. Never mention the product.
```
DO:   "The Wall Street executive who couldn't sleep..."
DO:   "They laughed when I sat down at the piano..."
DON'T: "Try our new sleep aid for executives"
```

**Copy structure:** Start with a story → reveal the problem → show the journey → introduce the solution category → position your product

### Level 2: Problem Aware
They know the problem but not the solution.

**Headline strategy:** Name and agitate the problem. Show you understand it.
```
DO:   "Tired of writing content that nobody reads?"
DO:   "Every founder hits this wall at $10K/month..."
DON'T: "Our tool helps you write better content"
```

**Copy structure:** Problem → agitation → consequences → "There's a better way" → introduce solution → proof → CTA

### Level 3: Solution Aware
They know solutions exist but don't know YOUR solution.

**Headline strategy:** Lead with the unique mechanism — HOW your solution works differently.
```
DO:   "The 'Reverse Funnel' method that converts 3x more than traditional funnels"
DO:   "Why AI-powered personalization is replacing A/B testing"
DON'T: "Buy our marketing tool"
```

**Copy structure:** Introduce mechanism → explain why it's different → prove it works → show who it works for → CTA

### Level 4: Product Aware
They know your product but haven't bought yet.

**Headline strategy:** Lead with proof, testimonials, case studies, or objection-handling.
```
DO:   "1,247 marketers switched to Marketing36z last quarter. Here's why."
DO:   "Marketing36z vs. [Competitor]: The honest comparison"
DON'T: "Introducing Marketing36z" (they already know you)
```

**Copy structure:** Strongest proof → specific results → objection handling → guarantee → urgency → CTA

### Level 5: Most Aware
They're ready to buy, just need a push.

**Headline strategy:** Lead with the deal — price, discount, deadline, bonus.
```
DO:   "Marketing36z — 50% off this week only. Includes the Conversion Kit bonus."
DO:   "Last 12 hours: Annual plan at the launch price"
DON'T: "What is Marketing36z?" (they already know — don't re-educate)
```

**Copy structure:** Offer → value stack → guarantee → urgency → CTA (no education needed)

## Part 4: Schwartz's Mass Desire Channeling Process

**Core axiom:** Copy cannot create desire. It can only take hopes, dreams, fears, and desires that already exist and focus them onto a particular product.

### Step 1: Choose the Most Powerful Desire
Every product appeals to multiple desires. Evaluate each on three dimensions:

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Urgency / Intensity** | How badly do they want to solve this? |
| **Staying Power** | How often does the desire recur? How hard to fully satisfy? |
| **Scope / Market Size** | How many people share this desire? |

Choose ONE desire to dominate the ad. This is the single most important decision.

### Step 2: Acknowledge the Desire in the Headline
The headline bridges the prospect's inner desire and your product.

### Step 3: Channel the Desire into Your Product
Body copy demonstrates with specificity and proof that your product satisfies the chosen desire.

## Part 4b: Schwartz's 38 Headline Verbalization Techniques

Schwartz proved you can take the same core claim and multiply its pulling power 5-50x by changing how you verbalize it. These 38 techniques are organized by function:

### A. Techniques That STRENGTHEN the Claim

| # | Technique | Description | Example |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | **Measure the size** | Add specific numbers for magnitude | "Lose weight" → "I am 61 pounds lighter" |
| 2 | **Measure the speed** | Add specific timeframes | "Feel better" → "In two seconds, Bayer aspirin begins to dissolve" |
| 3 | **Compare** | Show superiority through direct comparison | "Clean washes" → "Six times whiter washes" |
| 4 | **Metaphorize** | Turn benefit into a vivid visual metaphor | "Reduces fat" → "Melts away ugly fat" |
| 5 | **Sensitize** | Engage a specific sense (sight, touch, taste, smell) | "Lose weight" → "Feel lighter fast" |
| 6 | **Demonstrate by example** | Show one prime case in action | "Lose weight" → "It's just her diet!" |
| 7 | **Dramatize the result** | Turn into a mini-story or dramatic scene | "Lose weight" → "They all accused me of working out in secret..." |
| 8 | **State as a paradox** | Contradict expectations to create intrigue | "Lose weight" → "Lose weight by eating more!" |
| 9 | **Remove limitations** | Eliminate the expected sacrifice | "Lose weight" → "Lose weight fast without exercise" |
| 10 | **Associate with valued identity** | Link to admired people/groups | "Lose weight" → "Usain Bolt's diet secret" |

### B. Techniques That Add NOVELTY / FRESHNESS

| # | Technique | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 11 | **Twist the claim** | Present from an unexpected angle |
| 12 | **Turn into narration** | Convert from statement into a story |
| 13 | **Challenge with an example** | Dare the reader to test or disprove |
| 14 | **Contradict expectations** | Play with assumptions to create mystery |
| 15 | **Present from the opposite side** | Show the negative to highlight the positive |
| 16 | **Address the reader directly** | Make it personal and immediate |
| 17 | **Use the testimonial voice** | Let someone else state the claim |
| 18 | **Turn into a question** | Engage the brain's compulsion to answer |

### C. Techniques That CREATE PULL Into the Body

| # | Technique | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 19 | **Promise information** | "The secret of..." / "How to..." |
| 20 | **Question the reader** | Force mental engagement |
| 21 | **Partially reveal the mechanism** | Create a curiosity gap |
| 22 | **Feature the mechanism** | Make HOW-it-works the headline |
| 23 | **Offer a test or proof** | "Try this..." / "See for yourself..." |

### D. Additional Power Techniques

| # | Technique | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 24 | **Quantify precisely** | Specific numbers show scale ("47 checks") |
| 25 | **Inject speed/urgency** | Show how fast the benefit happens |
| 26 | **Be ultra-specific** | Specific > general. "61 pounds" > "lots of weight" |
| 27 | **Stretch the benefit in time** | Show long-term/cumulative impact |
| 28 | **Show how easy it is** | Remove perceived effort |
| 29 | **Bring in experts** | Authority endorsement in the headline |
| 30 | **Picture the black side** | Show what happens WITHOUT the product |

| 34 | **Offer a guarantee in the headline** | Remove risk upfront |
| 35 | **Name the audience** | Call out specifically who the ad is for |
| 36 | **Use news framing** | Present as a discovery or announcement |
| 37 | **Stack multiple benefits** | Layer more than one promise |
| 38 | **Invoke curiosity about the product** | Make the product itself mysterious |

**Usage rule:** Every headline should use at least 2-3 verbalization techniques layered together. The more techniques combined skillfully, the more powerful the headline.

## Part 4c: Schwartz's 7 Breakthrough Copy Techniques (Body Copy)

### Technique 1: INTENSIFICATION (13 Sub-Methods)
**Purpose:** Amplify desire until it overcomes skepticism, lethargy, and price resistance.

| # | Sub-Method | What It Does |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **First presentation** | Present the product bluntly with thorough, detailed description |
| 2 | **Put claims in action** | Show the product working in real scenarios, not just listing benefits |
| 3 | **Bring in the reader** | Give them a verbal demonstration of what happens to THEM day one |
| 4 | **Show how to test the claim** | Give the reader a way to verify — makes claims feel concrete |
| 5 | **Stretch benefits in time** | Show week one, month one, year one — compounding value |
| 6 | **Bring in an audience** | Show OTHER people reacting to the reader's transformation |
| 7 | **Show experts approving** | Professionals, celebrities, and sophisticates registering approval |
| 8 | **Compare and prove superiority** | Side-by-side: old disadvantages vs. new advantages |
| 9 | **Picture the black side** | Paint what happens WITHOUT the product (fear of loss) |
| 10 | **Show how easy it is** | The easier the benefit seems, the more desirable it becomes |
| 11 | **Use metaphor and imagination** | Translate abstract benefits into concrete, visual, emotional language |
| 12 | **Summarize before finishing** | Re-present all benefits in compressed form — cumulative wave of desire |
| 13 | **Put guarantee to work** | Turn guarantee into the climax — last brief summary reinforced by risk reversal |

### Technique 2: IDENTIFICATION
**Purpose:** Align the product with the prospect's self-image and aspirations.

Two kinds of roles people buy to fulfill:
- **Character roles:** Traits they want to embody ("progressive," "sophisticated," "smart")
- **Achievement roles:** Positions they want to hold ("leader," "expert," "good parent")

Two ways to leverage:
1. Product as **instrument for achieving** desired roles
2. Product as **acknowledgment** that desired roles have been achieved

### Technique 3: GRADUALIZATION (Belief Architecture)
**Purpose:** Make the prospect believe your big claim by building a chain of smaller accepted claims.

```
Start: Universally accepted truth (easy to believe)
  → Slightly bigger claim (accepted because #1 was)
    → Bigger claim (accepted because #2 was)
      → Your big claim (believed because the chain supports it)
```

**Key rule:** A claim's success depends entirely on the preparation made for it in preceding copy. Never make the target claim until the belief foundation supports it.

### Technique 4: REDEFINITION
**Purpose:** Overcome objections by reframing what the product is or means.

| Type | What It Does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Simplification** | Redefine as simpler than imagined | "It's just 3 clicks" |
| **Escalation** | Redefine value as greater than realized | "It's not a tool — it's a marketing department" |
| **Price reduction** | Redefine cost as smaller than it appears | "$49/month = $1.63/day = less than coffee" |

### Technique 5: MECHANIZATION (3 Stages)
**Purpose:** Prove your product works by revealing HOW.

| Stage | What to Do | When |
|-------|-----------|------|
| **1. Name it** | Give the process a proprietary, memorable name | Low sophistication markets |
| **2. Describe it** | Explain how it works in plain language | Medium sophistication |
| **3. Feature it** | Make the mechanism the central selling point and headline | High sophistication |

**Advanced — The Problem Mechanism:** When even solution mechanisms are saturated, define the previously unknown mechanism behind WHY the problem persists and why all other solutions fail. This reframes the entire category.

### Technique 6: CONCENTRATION
**Purpose:** Destroy the prospect's alternate ways of satisfying their desire.

Two structures:
1. **Parallels:** "Here's what they do poorly — here's how we do it better"
2. **Imaginary Before/After:** "Here's your life with old solutions — here's how much better with us"

**Critical rule:** If you can only attack competitors without showing YOUR superiority, say nothing.

### Technique 7: CAMOUFLAGE
**Purpose:** Borrow conviction from trusted sources and formats.

| Type | Method |
|------|--------|
| **Format** | Make the ad look and feel like editorial content |
| **Phraseology** | Use the language and jargon of the publication |
| **Understatement** | Simplicity implies truth — no superlatives, short sentences |
| **Deadly sincerity** | Openly acknowledge flaws so that stated benefits are believed more deeply |

---

### The Three Dimensions of All Body Copy (Schwartz)

Every piece of body copy must address three dimensions to create a complete "new world" in which the product is the inevitable solution:

1. **Desires** — What they want (Intensification handles this)
2. **Identifications** — Who they want to be (Identification handles this)
3. **Beliefs** — What they currently believe and what must change (Gradualization, Redefinition, Mechanization handle this)

## Part 5: Ogilvy's Rules for Copy

### Headlines
1. The headline is the most important element — five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy
2. Include the brand name in the headline
3. Headlines with news outperform headlines without
4. Headlines that promise a benefit outperform those that don't
5. Inject news into your headlines: "new," "announcing," "introducing"
6. Headlines of 6-12 words get the most engagement
7. Quotes in headlines increase recall by 28%
8. Don't use headlines as clever wordplay — clarity beats cleverness
9. Localize headlines when possible ("New in San Francisco...")
10. Headlines with specific numbers outperform vague claims
11. Headlines that arouse curiosity AND promise a benefit perform best

### Body Copy
1. Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs
2. Never write more than two sentences without starting a new paragraph
3. Use subheadings every few paragraphs (people scan before they read)
4. Include testimonials — they're more persuasive than anything the brand says
5. Write in conversational language — imagine you're writing to a friend
6. Specificity is credibility — "83.4% improvement" beats "significant improvement"
7. Long copy sells more than short copy (if every word earns its place)
8. The first paragraph must not exceed 11 words
9. Don't open with "we" or the company name — open with the reader
10. Never use superlatives ("best," "greatest") without proof

### The Ogilvy "Big Idea" Criteria
A Big Idea must pass all five tests:
1. **Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?** If not, it's not big enough.
2. **Do I wish I had thought of it myself?** If not, it's not original enough.
3. **Is it unique?** If not, it's not differentiated enough.
4. **Does it fit the strategy to perfection?** If not, it's off-brand.
5. **Could it be used for 30 years?** If not, it's a tactic, not a Big Idea.

## Part 6: Hopkins' Scientific Advertising Principles

### Principle 1: Track Everything
"Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly, and finally, by a test campaign." Every campaign is a test. Every test has a measurable outcome.

### Principle 2: The Specificity Doctrine
Vague claims mean nothing. Specific claims are remembered and believed.
```
VAGUE: "Our process makes shirts very clean"
SPECIFIC: "Our shirts go through 47 quality checks and a 14-step pressing process"
```

The number doesn't have to be round. "47 checks" is more believable than "50 checks" because it sounds counted, not invented.

### Principle 3: Offer Service, Not Salesmanship
"The best ads ask no one to buy. They are based entirely on service." Lead with what you're giving, not what you're asking for.

### Principle 4: Headlines Select Your Audience
"Use headlines to flag the people you want." Your headline should REPEL the wrong audience and ATTRACT the right one. Don't try to appeal to everyone.

### Principle 5: Tell the Full Story
"When you have a prospect's attention, tell them everything you want them to know. You won't get that attention again." Don't hold back — give them all the information they need to decide.

### Principle 6: Use Images That Earn Their Place
Never use an image for decoration. Every image must either:
- Show the product in use
- Show the transformation
- Show social proof
- Demonstrate a claim

## Part 7: Dan Kennedy's Direct Response Rules

### The 10 No-B.S. Rules
1. **There will always be an offer.** No piece of marketing without a clear ask.
2. **There will be a reason to respond right now.** Urgency is non-negotiable.
3. **There will be clear instructions.** "Call this number. Go to this URL. Click this button."
4. **There will be tracking and measurement.** If you can't measure it, don't run it.
5. **Follow-up matters more than the first touch.** 80% of sales happen after the 5th touch.
6. **Results rule. Period.** No awards for creativity. Only revenue matters.
7. **The message matters more than the media.** A great message on a bad channel beats a bad message on a great channel.
8. **Build a herd.** Create a community of loyal customers who buy repeatedly and refer others.
9. **Think in terms of customer lifetime value, not transaction value.** What's a customer worth over 3 years?
10. **Be willing to be boring.** Proven formats beat creative experiments most of the time.

### Kennedy's "Magnetic Marketing" System
```
Step 1: Create an irresistible lead magnet (free report, guide, assessment)
Step 2: Use it to attract pre-qualified prospects
Step 3: Follow up with a multi-step sequence (minimum 7 touches)
Step 4: Convert with a strong offer + guarantee + urgency
Step 5: Maximize customer value through upsells and referral systems
```

## Part 8: The Proof Stack Protocol

### How to Build an Unassailable Case for Your Product

Stack proof in this exact order for maximum persuasion:

```
Layer 1: Personal claim (weakest — but starts the narrative)
→ "We built this because we were frustrated with X"

Layer 2: Logic and reasoning
→ "Here's why traditional approaches fail: [specific reasoning]"

Layer 3: Statistics and data
→ "Companies using [method] see 47% higher conversion rates (Source: [study])"

Layer 4: Expert endorsement
→ "[Known expert] called this 'the most innovative approach to [category]'"

Layer 5: Specific case study
→ "[Customer Name] went from $5K to $25K/month in 90 days using this exact system"

Layer 6: Volume social proof
→ "Join 1,247 founders who've already made the switch"

Layer 7: Demonstration
→ "Watch this 2-minute demo and see it work in real-time"

Layer 8: Risk reversal (strongest — skin in the game)
→ "If you don't see results in 30 days, we'll refund every penny AND let you keep the bonuses"
```

**The Rule:** Every major claim in your copy must be supported by at least one proof element at Layer 3 or above. Claims at Layer 1-2 only are too weak.

This is the quality bar. Every piece that exits the Content Engine must have gone through all 10 steps.

## Part 10: Video Script Templates

### The Core Video Formats

Video follows different rules than written copy. Attention is measured in seconds, not paragraphs. Every script must earn the next 3 seconds.

#### Format 1: The Talking Head Script (60-90 seconds)

**Best for:** LinkedIn, YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Instagram Reels
**Awareness levels:** Problem Aware → Solution Aware

```
HOOK (0-3s):
"[Bold claim / surprising stat / contrarian statement]"

PROBLEM (3-15s):
"Most [audience] struggle with [problem] because [root cause].
They try [common approach] but it doesn't work because [reason]."

MECHANISM (15-35s):
"Here's what actually works: [unique approach/framework].
Step 1: [Action]
Step 2: [Action]
Step 3: [Action]"

PROOF (35-50s):
"[Specific result]. [Customer quote or personal example]."

CTA (50-60s):
"[Clear next step — follow, link in bio, comment, DM]"
```

#### Format 2: The Story Script (2-5 minutes)

**Best for:** YouTube, podcast clips, webinar openings
**Awareness levels:** Unaware → Problem Aware

```
HOOK (0-5s):
"[Surprising outcome or dramatic moment from the story]"

BACKSTORY (5-30s):
"[X months ago], I was [relatable starting point].
I was struggling with [problem the audience has too]."

THE WALL (30-60s):
"Everything changed when [crisis/failure moment].
I tried [approach 1], [approach 2], [approach 3] — nothing worked."

THE EPIPHANY (60-90s):
"Then I realized [key insight].
The problem wasn't [what most people think]. It was [the real issue]."

THE PLAN (90-150s):
"So I [what you did differently].
Here's the framework:
1. [Step with specific detail]
2. [Step with specific detail]
3. [Step with specific detail]"

THE RESULT (150-180s):
"Within [timeframe], [specific measurable outcome].
[Customer/personal proof point]."

THE BRIDGE (180-210s):
"You can do the same thing. Here's how to start:
[Simple first action they can take today]."

CTA (final):
"[Subscribe / follow / link / DM for resource]"
```

#### Format 3: The Tutorial Script (5-15 minutes)

**Best for:** YouTube, course content, webinar body
**Awareness levels:** Solution Aware → Product Aware

```
HOOK (0-10s):
"In the next [X] minutes, I'm going to show you exactly how to
[specific outcome]. No fluff, just the steps."

WHY THIS MATTERS (10-30s):
"[Audience] who master [this skill] see [specific benefit].
Most [audience] get it wrong because [common mistake]."

THE METHOD (30s-end):
"Step 1: [Action]
Here's exactly what that looks like: [demonstration/example]
Common mistake here: [what to avoid]

Step 2: [Action]
The key detail most people miss: [insider tip]
[Show example or result]

Step 3: [Action]
This is where [the transformation happens].
[Proof: before/after, metrics, customer result]"

RECAP (final 30-60s):
"To recap:
1. [Step 1 in 5 words]
2. [Step 2 in 5 words]
3. [Step 3 in 5 words]"

CTA:
"If you want [deeper resource / template / next step], [CTA].
Drop a comment with your biggest takeaway."
```

**Best for:** Ads, landing pages, social proof
**Awareness levels:** Product Aware → Most Aware

```
HOOK (0-3s):
"[Customer name]: '[Their most impressive result in one sentence]'"

THE BEFORE (3-15s):

"Now I [current state — specific metrics].
```

#### Format 5: The Ad Script (15-30 seconds)

**Best for:** Paid social ads (Meta, YouTube, TikTok)
**Awareness levels:** All (adapt hook to awareness level)

```
Cold Traffic (Problem Aware):
HOOK (0-3s): "[Pattern interrupt question or bold statement about their problem]"
AGITATE (3-10s): "[Expand on why the problem is worse than they think]"
SOLUTION (10-20s): "[Product] helps [audience] [achieve outcome] by [mechanism]."
PROOF (20-25s): "[Specific result or social proof number]"
CTA (25-30s): "[Clear action with urgency or risk reversal]"

HOOK (0-3s): "[Reference their previous interaction — 'Still thinking about...']"
PROOF (3-15s): "[Customer result or demo highlight]"
OBJECTION (15-22s): "[Handle their top objection — price, time, complexity]"
CTA (22-30s): "[Risk-reversed CTA — free trial, guarantee, limited offer]"
```

### Video Script Quality Rules

1. **The 3-Second Rule:** If the hook doesn't stop the scroll in 3 seconds, nothing else matters
2. **Say it, don't write it:** Scripts should sound like talking, not reading
3. **One idea per video:** Never cram multiple topics into one video
4. **Show, don't just tell:** Whenever possible, demonstrate rather than describe
5. **Captions are mandatory:** 85% of social video is watched without sound
6. **Pattern interrupt every 15-30 seconds:** Change visual, tone, or topic to retain attention
7. **End with value, not a beg:** "Here's what to do next" beats "Please like and subscribe"

## Part 11: Cold Email Copywriting

*Source: MarketingSkills `cold-email` skill*

### The Core Rule

> **Write like a peer, not a vendor.** Every sentence must earn its place. If it doesn't add value, delete it.

### Cold Email Structures

#### Structure 1: Observation-Problem-Proof-Ask
```
[Observation about their business — specific, shows research]
[Problem it implies — connects observation to a pain point]
[Proof you can solve it — one specific result]
[Soft ask — interest-based, not meeting request]
```

#### Structure 2: Question-Value-Ask
```
[Question that names their problem]
[1-2 sentences of specific value you deliver]
[Interest-based CTA]
```

#### Structure 3: Trigger-Insight-Ask
```
[Recent trigger event — funding, hire, launch, content they published]
[Insight connecting the trigger to a problem you solve]
[Soft ask]
```

### Subject Lines

- **2-4 words**, lowercase, no punctuation tricks
- Internal-looking beats salesy: "reply rates" > "Boost Your Revenue!!!"
- Examples: "quick question", "re: [their company]", "[mutual connection]", "idea for [company]"
- **Never:** fake Re:/Fwd:, ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, "I hope this finds you well"

### Follow-Up Cadence

| Touch | Timing | Rule |
|:-----:|--------|------|
| 1 | Day 0 | Initial email — observation + value + soft ask |
| 2 | Day 3 | Add something new — case study, data point, or different angle |
| 3 | Day 7 | Different approach — shorter, new insight, or question |
| 4 | Day 14 | Final value-add — resource, insight, or breakup |
| 5 | Day 21 | Breakup email — "Should I close the loop?" |

**Rules:**
- Each follow-up adds something NEW (never "just checking in")
- 3-5 total emails maximum
- Increasing gaps between touches
- Interest-based CTAs ("Worth exploring?") beat meeting requests

### Voice Calibration by Audience

| Audience | Tone | Length | CTA Style |
|----------|------|--------|-----------|
| C-suite | Ultra-brief, peer-level | 2-3 sentences | "Worth a conversation?" |
| Mid-level | More specific value | 3-5 sentences | "Want me to send a quick overview?" |
| Technical | Precise, no fluff | 3-4 sentences | "Happy to share the technical approach" |

### What to NEVER Do in Cold Email
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- Feature dumps
- HTML templates or images
- Fake Re: or Fwd: subjects
- Asking for a 30-minute call in first touch
- Generic personalization ("I saw your LinkedIn post" without connecting it to a problem)

### 14.1 SCAMPER (Creative Adaptation)

Apply to any existing marketing asset to generate variations:

| Letter | Technique | Marketing Application |
|:------:|-----------|---------------------|
| **S** | Substitute | Replace one element (channel, format, audience, offer) |

| **M** | Modify/Magnify | Change scale, scope, or emphasis |
| **P** | Put to other uses | Repurpose for different audience or channel |
| **E** | Eliminate | Remove elements to simplify |

### 14.2 SIT — Systematic Inventive Thinking (5 Templates)

| Template | How It Works | Marketing Example |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Subtraction** | Remove a core component | Remove the pricing page → "Name your own price" |
| **Multiplication** | Duplicate and modify a component | Multiple CTAs, each for different awareness levels |

| **Task Unification** | Assign new tasks to existing elements | Turn customer support into content creation |
| **Attribute Dependency** | Create dependency between variables | Pricing that changes based on usage/results |

### 14.3 Bisociation (Koestler's Concept)

**The creative act:** Connecting two previously unrelated frames of reference.

**Application to marketing:**
1. List 10 things your product does
2. List 10 things from a completely unrelated domain (cooking, sports, music, nature)
3. Force connections between each pair
4. Best connections become campaign concepts

**Example:** Marketing OS + Orchestra = "Your marketing team, perfectly conducted" (orchestration metaphor that became the Orchestrator agent name)

### 14.4 The Random Input Technique (De Bono)

1. Pick a random word from a dictionary
2. List 5 associations with that word
3. Connect each association to your marketing challenge
4. Evaluate the unexpected ideas generated

### 14.5 Creative Evaluation Rubric

Before shipping any creative, score it:

| Dimension | 1 (Weak) | 3 (Average) | 5 (Exceptional) |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Originality** | Seen it before | Fresh angle | Never seen this approach |
| **Relevance** | Loose connection to audience | Addresses their world | Deeply personal to their situation |
| **Clarity** | Confusing, multiple messages | Clear but forgettable | Crystal clear AND memorable |
| **Emotion** | Flat, informational only | Some feeling | Strong emotional response |
| **Action** | Unclear next step | Standard CTA | Irresistible, obvious next step |
| **Shareability** | Nobody would forward this | Some might share | People would feel compelled to share |

**Minimum bar:** Average 3.0 across all dimensions. Any dimension below 2 = rework required.

## Part 15: Copy Testing & Optimization

### 15.1 The 5 Copy Tests (Apply Before Publishing)

| Test | Question | How to Apply |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **The Delete Test** | Does removing this sentence make the piece weaker? | If no, delete it |
| **The Bar Stool Test** | Would you say this to a friend at a bar? | If not, rewrite it |
| **The So What Test** | Does the reader care about this? | If not, reframe as benefit |
| **The Forehead Slap Test** | Does the headline make you think "I need to read this"? | If not, rewrite the headline |
| **The Grandmother Test** | Would your grandmother understand this? | If not, simplify |

### 15.2 Copy Review Checklist (25-Point)

**Structure:**
- [ ] One single big idea (not multiple competing ideas)
- [ ] Headline passes the 4U test (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific)
- [ ] Opening line creates forward momentum (Sugarman Slide)
- [ ] Every paragraph transitions to the next
- [ ] One primary CTA (not competing CTAs)

**Persuasion:**
- [ ] Addresses one specific desire or pain
- [ ] Features translated to benefits to emotional outcomes
- [ ] Proof stacked at Level 3+ (data, expert, case study)
- [ ] Objections addressed before CTA
- [ ] Risk reversed (guarantee, free trial, easy cancellation)

**Clarity:**
- [ ] No jargon without explanation
- [ ] Specificity over vagueness (numbers, names, details)
- [ ] Active voice throughout
- [ ] Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- [ ] Subheadings every 3-4 paragraphs

**Voice:**
- [ ] Consistent tone throughout
- [ ] Reads like conversation, not corporate speak
- [ ] "You" language dominates (not "we")
- [ ] Sensory and emotional words present
- [ ] Brand voice guidelines followed

**Technical:**
- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors
- [ ] Links work and point to correct destinations
- [ ] Character limits respected (if platform-specific)
- [ ] Mobile-readable (short lines, scannable)
- [ ] Accessibility considered (alt text, contrast)
