# Category Design Playbook

## Why Category Design Matters

Category kings capture 76% of the total value in their market (Play Bigger research). If you're competing in someone else's category, you're playing their game by their rules. Category design lets you define a new game where you're the default winner.

---

## Part 1: Should You Create a Category?

### Decision Framework

```
Is your product fundamentally different from existing solutions?
├── YES → Does it solve a problem people don't have a name for yet?
│   ├── YES → Does the market need a new mental model to understand the value?
│   │   ├── YES → STRONG candidate for category design
│   │   └── NO  → Consider sub-category or positioning refresh
│   └── NO  → Consider sub-category positioning
└── NO  → Compete within existing category, focus on differentiation
```

### Additional Qualification Criteria

| Criterion | Yes = Category Candidate | No = Compete in Existing |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| Existing category name feels wrong or limiting | ✓ | |
| Customers struggle to compare you to competitors | ✓ | |
| Your product requires a fundamentally different buying process | ✓ | |
| You're often in "no decision" rather than losing to competitors | ✓ | |
| The "before" and "after" of using your product is transformational | ✓ | |
| You need to educate the market before they understand the value | ✓ | |

**Warning:** Category design is expensive (time, effort, market education). Don't create a category for a feature or a slight variation. Only do it for genuinely transformational approaches.

## Part 2: The Play Bigger Framework

Category design has three interdependent elements:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           CATEGORY DESIGN               │
│                                         │
│    Company ←──→ Product ←──→ Category   │
│    Design       Design       Design     │
│                                         │
│    WHO you      WHAT you     WHY the    │
│    are          build        market     │
│                              needs      │
│                              this       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Element | What It Covers | Key Questions |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| **Company Design** | Culture, mission, values, org structure | Who are we? What do we believe? |
| **Product Design** | Features, experience, architecture | What do we build that's different? |
| **Category Design** | Market definition, problem framing, naming | Why does this category need to exist? |

**All three must align.** A new category without a differentiated product is just marketing. A differentiated product without a category is confusing to buyers.

## Part 3: Category Naming

### 7 Rules for Category Names

| Rule | Explanation | Good Example | Bad Example |
|------|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Short** | 2-3 words maximum | Revenue Intelligence | AI-Powered Conversational Revenue Operations Platform |
| **Memorable** | Easy to say and remember | Inbound Marketing | Customer-Centric Demand Generation |
| **Descriptive** | Hints at what it does | Conversational Marketing | Digital Engagement Suite |
| **Ownable** | Not a generic term | Revenue Intelligence (Gong) | Sales Software |
| **Future-proof** | Won't become outdated | Cloud Computing | Web 2.0 Applications |
| **Inclusive** | Room for the category to grow | Marketing Automation | Email Marketing Software |
| **Not an acronym** | Humans need to say it | Customer Data Platform | CDP (initially confusing) |

### Category Naming Exercise

1. Write down the OLD way of solving this problem in 2-3 words
2. Write down YOUR approach in 2-3 words
3. The category name should capture the GAP between old and new
4. Test: Can someone who's never heard it guess roughly what it means?
5. Test: Would a journalist use this term in a headline?

## Part 4: The POV Lightning Strike Document

The Lightning Strike is the foundational document that defines your category's worldview. It's not a product pitch — it's a market manifesto.

### 7-Section Template

#### Section 1: The Undeniable Shift
**What's changing in the world that makes the old approach obsolete?**

"The world is shifting from [old paradigm] to [new paradigm]. This shift is being driven by [2-3 macro forces: technology changes, buyer behavior changes, market dynamics]. Companies that recognize this shift will [positive outcome]. Companies that don't will [negative outcome]."

*Example (HubSpot):* "Buyers have fundamentally changed how they discover and evaluate products. They don't want to be interrupted by cold calls and banner ads — they want to find solutions on their own terms. The shift from outbound to inbound is irreversible."

#### Section 2: Winners and Losers
**Who benefits from this shift and who gets left behind?**

| Winners | Losers |
|---------|--------|
| Companies that [new approach] | Companies stuck in [old approach] |
| Teams that embrace [new capability] | Teams that rely on [outdated method] |
| Leaders who invest in [new category] | Leaders who double down on [legacy] |

#### Section 3: The Promised Land
**What does the future look like for companies that adopt this new approach?**

"Imagine a world where [positive vision]. Where [specific benefit 1]. Where [specific benefit 2]. Where [specific benefit 3]. This is the promised land of [category name]."

**Rule:** Describe the outcome, NOT your product. The promised land is about the customer's transformed state.

#### Section 4: The Gap
**Why can't existing solutions deliver the promised land?**

"Traditional [old category] was built for [old world]. It assumes [outdated assumption 1], [outdated assumption 2], and [outdated assumption 3]. In a world of [new reality], these assumptions break down."

#### Section 5: The New Approach
**What is [category name] and why is it different?**

"[Category name] is a fundamentally different approach to [problem space]. Unlike [old category], which [old approach], [category name] [new approach]."

| Old Category | Your Category |
|-------------|--------------|
| Does [old thing 1] | Does [new thing 1] |
| Assumes [old assumption] | Designed for [new reality] |
| Optimizes for [old metric] | Optimizes for [new metric] |
| Requires [old effort] | Enables [new efficiency] |

#### Section 6: Magic Gifts
**What capabilities does your product provide to reach the promised land?**

These are your product features, framed as tools that help the hero (customer) reach the promised land:

1. **[Capability 1]:** Enables [outcome] by [how it works]
2. **[Capability 2]:** Enables [outcome] by [how it works]
3. **[Capability 3]:** Enables [outcome] by [how it works]

**Rule:** Features are "magic gifts" — they help the hero on their journey. They are NOT the hero of the story.

#### Section 7: Proof
**Evidence that the promised land is real and achievable.**

| Proof Type | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Customer results | "[Company] achieved [specific result] in [timeframe]" |
| Market data | "[X]% of [audience] have already adopted [approach]" |
| Expert endorsement | "[Analyst/influencer] says '[quote about category]'" |
| Company trajectory | "[Your company] has grown [X]% since pioneering [category]" |

## Part 5: Andy Raskin's Strategic Narrative (5-Step)

A simpler, complementary framework for category storytelling:

### Step 1: Name an Undeniable Shift
Open with a change in the world that creates both opportunity and threat. This is NOT about your product — it's about the world.

*Example (Zuora):* "We've entered the Subscription Economy. The way people consume products and services has fundamentally changed."

### Step 2: Show There Will Be Winners and Losers
Create stakes. If the audience doesn't feel urgency, they won't pay attention.

*Example:* "Companies that adapt to this shift are growing 5x faster than those clinging to the old model."

### Step 3: Tease the Promised Land
Describe the future state — what life looks like for winners. Make it aspirational but believable.

*Example:* "Imagine knowing exactly how much revenue you'll generate next month, next quarter, and next year — with 95% accuracy."

### Step 4: Introduce Magic Gifts
Now (and only now) introduce your product capabilities as the tools that get customers to the promised land.

*Example:* "To get there, you need three things: [capability 1], [capability 2], and [capability 3]. That's exactly what [product] provides."

### Step 5: Present Evidence
Prove that the promised land is real with customer stories, data, and results.

*Example:* "[Customer A] increased subscription revenue by 340%. [Customer B] reduced churn by 60%. [Industry analyst] calls this 'the most important shift in business models since SaaS.'"

## Part 6: Category Evangelism Content Plan

### Content Types for Category Creation

| Content Type | Purpose | Frequency | Owner |
|-------------|---------|:---------:|-------|
| **Category manifesto** (blog/video) | Define the shift and the promised land | Once (update annually) | Strategy Planner |
| **Research report** | Prove the category with data | Quarterly | Content Engine |
| **Category comparison** | Old way vs. new way | Monthly | Content Engine |
| **Customer stories** | Proof of the promised land | Monthly | Content Engine |
| **Founder POV content** | Thought leadership on the shift | Weekly | Social Commander |
| **Analyst briefings** | Get analysts to recognize the category | Quarterly | Strategy Planner |
| **Speaking/events** | Evangelize the category to the industry | Monthly | Founder |
| **Glossary/education** | Help market understand new terms | Once (update quarterly) | Content Engine |

### Category Education Sequence

| Stage | Audience Awareness | Content Focus | CTA |
|:-----:|-------------------|---------------|-----|
| 1 | "I don't know this category exists" | The undeniable shift (problems with old way) | Read/share/discuss |
| 2 | "I'm curious about this new approach" | What the category IS and why it matters | Download guide |
| 3 | "I understand the category but haven't tried it" | How to evaluate solutions, success stories | Free trial/demo |
| 4 | "I'm evaluating options in this category" | Why you're the category leader, comparisons | Sales conversation |
| 5 | "I've chosen you" | Onboarding, education, expansion | Adopt fully |

## Part 7: Measuring Category Adoption

| Metric | What to Track | Target |
|--------|-------------|:------:|
| **Branded category search** | Google searches for "[your category name]" | Growing quarter-over-quarter |
| **Category mentions in media** | Press articles using your category name | 5+ mentions/quarter |
| **Analyst recognition** | Analyst reports referencing your category | At least 1 major analyst within 18 months |
| **Competitor adoption** | Competitors using your category name | 2+ competitors adopt the term |
| **Wikipedia/Knowledge panel** | Category has its own Wikipedia entry | Within 2-3 years |
| **Community usage** | People use the category name organically in forums, social | Organic adoption growing |
| **AI recognition** | AI search engines use your category name | Within 1-2 years of consistent use |
| **Job postings** | Companies posting jobs with your category in the title | Ultimate validation |

## Part 8: Case Studies

### Salesforce: CRM → Cloud Computing
- **Before:** CRM was on-premise software (Siebel, SAP)
- **Shift named:** "No Software" → Cloud Computing
- **Category created:** SaaS / Cloud CRM
- **Result:** Defined and dominated the category for 20+ years

### HubSpot: Digital Marketing → Inbound Marketing
- **Before:** Marketing was about outbound interruption
- **Shift named:** Buyers have changed, marketers must too
- **Category created:** Inbound Marketing
- **Result:** The term "inbound marketing" gets 40K+ monthly searches

### Drift: Live Chat → Conversational Marketing
- **Before:** Website chat was a support tool
- **Shift named:** Buyers want conversations, not forms
- **Category created:** Conversational Marketing
- **Result:** Grew from $0 to $100M+ ARR, acquired by Salesloft

### Gong: Call Recording → Revenue Intelligence
- **Before:** Sales calls were recorded but not analyzed
- **Shift named:** Revenue teams are flying blind
- **Category created:** Revenue Intelligence
- **Result:** Category adopted by analysts, competitors, and the market

### Lessons from These Cases
1. The shift must be REAL and undeniable (not manufactured)
2. The category name must be intuitive and sticky
3. The company must invest heavily in education (years, not months)
4. Early adopters validate the category before the market accepts it
5. Eventually, competitors adopt your category name — that's when you've won

## Part 9: Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | What to Do Instead |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Naming a feature as a category** | Too narrow to sustain a market | Name the outcome or approach, not the technology |
| **Creating a category no one needs** | Market doesn't feel the problem | Validate the shift with customer research first |
| **Premature category design** | Not enough product-market fit | Get PMF first, then define the category |
| **Category without differentiated product** | Just marketing spin | Align product, company, and category design |
| **Giving up too early** | Category creation takes 2-3 years | Commit for the long haul or don't start |
| **Too complex a name** | Nobody can remember or say it | 2-3 words, intuitive, memorable |
| **Not investing in education** | Market can't evaluate what it doesn't understand | Budget for extensive content, events, analyst relations |
| **Letting competitors define you** | Reacting instead of leading | Proactively define the category narrative |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Category kings capture 76% of value.** Being #1 in a new category beats being #3 in an existing one.
2. **Category design starts with a shift, not a product.** Name the change in the world first.
3. **The promised land is about the customer, not you.** Your product is a "magic gift," not the hero.
4. **Naming matters enormously.** 2-3 words, memorable, ownable, future-proof.
5. **It takes 2-3 years.** Category design is a long game — commit or don't start.
6. **Education is the strategy.** You're not just selling — you're teaching the market a new way to think.
7. **When competitors adopt your term, you've won.** That's the ultimate validation.
