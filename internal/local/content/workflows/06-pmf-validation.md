# Workflow: Product-Market Fit Validation

## Purpose
Systematically validate whether your product solves a real problem for a specific market before investing in marketing. Most marketing fails not because of bad copy or wrong channels — it fails because the product doesn't have product-market fit. This workflow prevents wasted marketing spend on products the market doesn't want.

## When to Use This Workflow
- Founder is pre-revenue and unsure if the market wants their product
- First 10 Customers Playbook flagged <3/5 on the "Will They Pay?" test
- Founder has a product but can't articulate why customers buy (or don't)
- Churn is high and founder suspects a product problem, not a marketing problem
- Orchestrator identifies fundamental product-market questions before any campaign work

---

## Phase 1: Define What You're Testing

### Step 1.1: The PMF Hypothesis

Write your hypothesis in this format:

```
I believe [specific customer segment]
has a [frequency: daily/weekly/monthly] problem with [specific problem]
and would pay [$X] for a solution that [specific outcome].

Evidence supporting this belief:
1. [What you've observed/heard]
2. [Data point or research]
3. [Customer conversation or market signal]

Evidence against this belief:
1. [Counterargument or risk]
2. [Why this might not work]
```

### Step 1.2: Risk Assessment

Rate each PMF risk on a 1-5 scale (1 = low risk, 5 = high risk):

| Risk Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|---------------|:-----:|---------|
| **Desirability** — Do people want this? | /5 | |
| **Viability** — Will they pay enough? | /5 | |
| **Feasibility** — Can you deliver it? | /5 | |
| **Usability** — Can they actually use it? | /5 | |
| **Differentiation** — Why you vs. alternatives? | /5 | |

**Priority:** Test the highest-risk dimension first.

## Phase 2: Customer Discovery Interviews

### Step 2.1: Who to Interview

| Segment | Count | Why |
|---------|:-----:|-----|
| People who match your ICP and have the problem | 5-8 | Core validation |
| People who tried competing solutions | 3-5 | Understand alternatives |
| People who match ICP but DON'T have the problem | 2-3 | Validate problem prevalence |

**Total:** 10-15 interviews minimum for signal.

### Step 2.2: The Interview Framework (Mom Test)

**Core rule (Rob Fitzpatrick):** Never ask "Would you use this?" or "Do you like this idea?" People lie to be polite. Instead, ask about their PAST BEHAVIOR and CURRENT SITUATION.

#### Questions That Reveal Truth

**About the Problem:**
1. "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem area]. What happened?"
2. "How are you solving this today?" (Current alternatives)
3. "What's the most annoying part of your current approach?"
4. "How much time/money does this cost you per [week/month]?"
5. "What have you tried before that didn't work? Why?"

**About Urgency:**
6. "When was the last time this problem cost you money/time/a customer?"
7. "If you could snap your fingers and fix this, what would change?"
8. "Where does solving this rank in your priorities right now?" (Top 3 or not?)

**About Willingness to Pay:**
9. "Have you ever paid for a solution to this? How much?"
10. "If something solved [specific outcome], what would that be worth to you?"
11. "What's your budget for tools/services in this area?"

**About the Buying Process:**
12. "If you decided to buy something like this, what would you do first?"
13. "Who else would need to approve this decision?"
14. "What would make you nervous about trying a new solution?"

#### Interview Anti-Patterns

| Don't Do This | Do This Instead |
|--------------|----------------|
| "Would you buy this?" | "Have you ever paid for something like this?" |
| "Do you like this idea?" | "Tell me about the last time you dealt with this" |
| "What if it could do X?" | "What do you wish your current solution did?" |
| "Would you pay $49/mo?" | "What are you currently spending on this?" |
| Pitch your solution | Listen. Take notes. Ask follow-ups. |

### Step 2.3: Signal vs. Noise Analysis

After 10-15 interviews, look for these patterns:

| Strong Signal (PMF likely) | Weak Signal (PMF unlikely) |
|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| 8+ people describe the same problem | Everyone describes a different problem |
| They're actively spending money on alternatives | "It would be nice" but no current spend |
| They get emotional describing the pain | Polite but dispassionate responses |
| They ask when they can buy/try it | They say "sounds interesting" and change subject |
| They offer to refer others | They can't think of anyone else who'd want it |
| They describe failed attempts to solve it | They haven't tried to solve it |

## Phase 3: The Sean Ellis PMF Test

### Step 3.1: The 40% Rule

If you already have users (even free), ask this single question:

> "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
> - Very disappointed
> - Somewhat disappointed
> - Not disappointed
> - N/A — I no longer use it

**Benchmark:** If 40%+ say "Very disappointed," you have PMF.

| Score | Interpretation | Next Step |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| >40% Very Disappointed | **PMF achieved** | Scale marketing confidently |
| 25-40% Very Disappointed | **Close to PMF** | Interview "very disappointed" users, double down on what they love |
| <25% Very Disappointed | **No PMF yet** | Interview "somewhat disappointed" users, find what's missing |

### Step 3.2: Follow-Up Questions (for "Very Disappointed" respondents)

1. "What is the primary benefit you receive from [product]?"
2. "What type of person do you think would benefit most?"
3. "How can we improve [product] for you?"

These answers become your:
- **Positioning** (primary benefit → core promise)
- **ICP refinement** (who benefits most → target customer)
- **Roadmap input** (how to improve → product priorities)

## Phase 4: Demand Validation Experiments

### Step 4.1: The Landing Page Test

Before building anything, test demand with a landing page:

| Element | What to Include |
|---------|----------------|
| Headline | Core promise (Problem → Outcome) |
| Subheadline | How it works (1 sentence) |
| 3 benefit bullets | Outcome-focused, not feature-focused |
| CTA | "Join the waitlist" or "Get early access" |
| Social proof | Even "Built by [your credentials]" counts early on |

**Metrics that matter:**

| Metric | PMF Signal | No-PMF Signal |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Landing page → email signup | >10% conversion | <3% conversion |
| Email signup → reply to welcome email | >5% reply rate | <1% reply rate |
| Waitlist → pays when launched | >15% conversion | <5% conversion |

### Step 4.2: The Pre-Sale Test

The strongest PMF signal is someone paying before you build:

| Pre-Sale Method | How It Works | PMF Confirmed If... |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Founding member offer** | Sell access at a discount before launch | 10+ people pay |
| **Crowdfunding** | Campaign on relevant platform | Fully funded |
| **Letter of intent** | B2B prospects sign LOI | 3+ signed LOIs |
| **Pilot program** | Offer paid pilot with defined scope | 5+ companies sign up |
| **Manual delivery** | Sell the outcome, deliver manually first | Customers are satisfied and renew |

### Step 4.3: The Smoke Test Menu

For ideas you're not sure about, run these quick experiments:

| Test | Cost | Time | What It Proves |
|------|------|------|---------------|
| Google Ads to landing page | $100-300 | 1 week | Search demand exists |
| LinkedIn poll | Free | 24 hours | Topic resonance |
| Community post asking about the problem | Free | 48 hours | Problem prevalence |
| Cold DMs offering to solve the problem | Free | 1 week | Willingness to engage |
| Competitor review mining | Free | 2-3 hours | Gaps in existing solutions |

## Phase 5: PMF Scorecard

### The Comprehensive Assessment

After completing Phases 2-4, score your PMF status:

| Dimension | Evidence Required | Score |
|-----------|------------------|:-----:|
| **Problem validation** | 8+ of 15 interviewees describe the same problem | /10 |
| **Solution validation** | 5+ interviewees would use/pay for your approach | /10 |
| **Willingness to pay** | Price point validated through conversations or pre-sales | /10 |
| **Urgency** | Problem is in their top 3 priorities | /10 |
| **Differentiation** | Clear reason to choose you over alternatives | /10 |
| **Sean Ellis score** | % who would be "very disappointed" | /10 |
| **Demand signal** | Landing page conversion, pre-sales, waitlist size | /10 |
| **Organic pull** | People asking for it without prompting | /10 |

### PMF Decision Framework

| Total Score | PMF Status | What to Do |
|:-----------:|-----------|-----------|
| 65-80 | **Strong PMF** | Scale marketing aggressively → Growth & Retention playbook |
| 50-64 | **Emerging PMF** | Double down on "very disappointed" segment → First 10 Customers |
| 35-49 | **Partial PMF** | Narrow your ICP, pivot your positioning, retest |
| 20-34 | **Weak PMF** | Major pivot needed — different problem, audience, or solution |
| <20 | **No PMF** | Stop. New hypothesis. Return to Phase 1. |

## Phase 6: PMF → Marketing Handoff

When PMF is confirmed (score 50+), package these artifacts for the marketing system:

### Handoff Checklist

| Artifact | Source | Destination |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Validated ICP (with real data, not assumptions) | Interview data | Strategy Planner |
| Customer Language Bank (exact words from interviews) | Interview transcripts | Content Engine |
| Core promise (what "very disappointed" users value most) | Sean Ellis follow-up | Positioning/Big Idea |
| Pricing validation (what they'll pay, anchored to alternatives) | Payment data | Offer Design Lab |
| Top 3 objections (real, from interviews) | Interview notes | Copy/Proof Stack |
| Competitive alternatives (what they actually use today) | Interview data | Dunford Positioning |
| Distribution channel (where these people hang out) | Interview data | Channel Operator |
| First proof points (early results, testimonials) | Customer data | Quality Guardian |
